Abstract

Decentralized systems require governance norms that emerge from agent dynamics, evolve with community needs, resist metric gaming, and maintain formal guarantees. No existing framework provides all four properties simultaneously with proofs. This dissertation introduces Version 4.0 of Randall's Feedback (RF), a formal coordination and validation framework for emergence-first governance with adaptive goal evolution and convergent validation. RF treats governance as a dynamical system in which norms emerge from collective free energy minimization rather than top-down design.

We present six formal contributions. Theorem V4-1 (RF Governance Convergence) establishes that agents satisfying the particular partition conditions of Friston et al. (2023) converge asymptotically to a governance attractor through Hamiltonian matching with the shared governance protocol, drawing on Isomura's (2023) Bayesian mechanics and Tull et al.'s (2023) categorical compositionality theorems. Theorem V4-2 (Collective Goal-Update Admissibility) provides necessary and sufficient conditions for a collection of individually admissible goal-update operators to be collectively stable, formalized via Banach fixed-point contraction on the product space of Metelli et al.'s (2023) feasible reward sets. Theorem V4-3 (Dynamic Goodhart Resistance) proves that the contribution-metric/governance-goal discrepancy process remains light-tailed under bounded goal-update rates and Lipschitz metric continuity, invoking El-Mhamdi and Hoang's (2024) tail-distribution characterization of Goodhart dynamics. Theorem V4-4 (Unhackability Under Constrained Policy Classes) shows that RF's admissibility conditions restrict the contribution strategy space to a finite set, enabling non-trivial unhackable metric-goal pairs per Skalse et al. (2022). Theorem V4-5 (Convergent Validation Without Ground Truth) extends the Capture-the-Flag Peer Prediction mechanism of Zhao et al. (2024) to n validators with linear collusion tolerance. Conjecture V4-6 proposes that RF achieves an approximate resolution of the MeritRank Generalizability-Trustlessness-Sybil-Resistance trilemma through epsilon-relaxation of Sybil resistance.

Together, these results provide the first formal framework combining Free Energy Principle-grounded convergence, Bayesian goal evolution with sample complexity bounds, dynamic Goodhart resistance under non-stationary goals, and convergent validation without ground truth. We present a complete empirical validation framework with five prioritized experimental programs and identify open problems including convergence rate characterization and the resolution of collective expected free energy minimization failures in strategic multi-agent interactions.

1. Introduction

1.1 The Governance Problem in Decentralized Systems
Decentralized systems—from blockchain-based DAOs to open-source software communities—face a fundamental governance challenge. As of 2024, the DAO ecosystem encompasses over 13,000 organizations managing approximately $24.5 billion in treasury assets across 11.1 million governance token holders. These systems require governance norms that (i) emerge organically from the dynamics of participating agents rather than being imposed by centralized authorities, (ii) evolve adaptively as community needs, external conditions, and strategic landscapes shift over time, (iii) resist metric gaming, reward hacking, and other forms of Goodhart dynamics that inevitably arise when agents optimize proxy measures, and (iv) maintain formal mathematical guarantees of convergence, stability, and incentive compatibility throughout this adaptive process.

The empirical evidence is clear that top-down governance design fails at scale. A comprehensive analysis of 100 DAOs demonstrates that grassroots participation—not concentrated voting power or designer-specified rules—is the strongest predictor of governance efficiency. Complexity science approaches to DAO governance have identified mechanisms from collective intelligence research, including stigmergy, quorum sensing, and diversity maintenance, that improve governance outcomes precisely because they arise from agent-level dynamics rather than central planning. Yet the formal theory has not kept pace with practice: no existing framework provides convergence proofs for decentralized governance mechanisms beyond static equilibrium existence.

1.2 The RF Framework: Overview
Randall's Feedback (RF) is a formal coordination and validation framework for emergence-first governance. It is not a grand unified theory; rather, RF provides a mathematical layer that operates alongside existing frameworks—the Free Energy Principle provides agent models, reinforcement learning provides optimization tools, cybernetics provides stability analysis, and mechanism design provides incentive analysis. RF adds three capabilities absent from any single existing framework: (1) formal goal evolution operators with convergence guarantees, (2) bootstrap governance that transitions from contribution-only to reputation-weighted validation, and (3) convergent validation without ground truth in multi-agent settings.

1.3 Key Contributions of V4
Version 4 presents six formal contributions:
- Theorem V4-1 (RF Governance Convergence): Agents satisfy particular partition conditions and converge to a governance attractor through Hamiltonian matching.
- Theorem V4-2 (Collective Goal-Update Admissibility): Necessary and sufficient conditions for collective goal update stability.
- Theorem V4-3 (Dynamic Goodhart Resistance): Discrepancy process remains light-tailed under bounded updates.
- Theorem V4-4 (Unhackability Under Constrained Policy Classes): Strategy space restriction enables unhackable pairs.
- Theorem V4-5 (Convergent Validation Without Ground Truth): Byzantine-robust peer prediction for n validators.
- Conjecture V4-6 (MeritRank Trilemma Resolution): Approximate resolution of the MeritRank trilemma via Sybil yield bounds.
